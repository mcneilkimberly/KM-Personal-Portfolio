Crossed Groups¶

Identify groups of features in your data that should be combined into cross-product features. Provide a compelling justification for why these features should be crossed (or why some features should not be crossed).

There are many features that shouldn't be crossed, and they can fall into a few different categories:

  1. Do not cross two features within the same one-hot coded group, as they are mutually exclusive.
  2. Do not cross anything with the target feature (any of the four weather types), as you can't exactly predict your output variable with your output variable. That would invalidate our models.
  3. Do not cross too many features, as that can lead to overfitting because of how specific the predictions will become.
  4. Do not cross features that have little to no rational relationship, as whatever the result is would not be very beneficial for prediction purposes.

Cloud Cover and Precipitation can be crossed, as certain cloud types, such as overcast, are more associated with rain and snow, while clear skies are more associated with sunny weather.

Evaluation Metric¶

Choose and explain what metric(s) you will use to evaluate your algorithm’s performance. You should give a detailed argument for why this (these) metric(s) are appropriate on your data. That is, why is the metric appropriate for the task (e.g., in terms of the business case for the task). Please note: rarely is accuracy the best evaluation metric to use. Think deeply about an appropriate measure of performance.

Some evaluation metrics that would be beneficial for evaluating our algorithm's performance include

  1. Precision - Of all the positive predictions, how many were actually positive?
  2. F1-Score - Weighted look at Percision and Recall, best for when the cost of a false positive and a false negative are very different.
  3. Recall - of all the correct guesses (positive and negative), how many were positive?

Note: Just to cover more bases, we will also be finding our models' accuracies; however, we will not be using it to evaluate which model is best.

Of all the metrics for evaluation, we believe that Precision is the best measure for our task. A model like this would be best used to report on the weather in the moment, for when someone looks at the weather to determine what to wear or do that day. In this multiclassification problem there is a much stronger emphasis on correct positives than correct negatives. It isn't like a problem where you identify whether something is or not, all of the instances will have a correct class, so true positives are much more important than true negatives, and false positives are the main concern. Therefore, Recall isn't a very good metric to judge to model by. Precision is the best. It can be calculated per class and averaged to get an overall score, while also looking at the different scores to see if the model falters more on one class than the others. It makes sense as false positives in this context mean the model incorrectly predicted it was another kind of weather, which would be bad for the consumer. Therefore, precision gives the best look into how often the model "gets it right". We don't believe F1-Score is a good measurement in this case as again, recall doesn't make much sense here, so it taking Recall into account doesn't help.

Data Split¶

Choose the method you will use for dividing your data into training and testing (i.e., are you using Stratified 10-fold cross validation? Shuffle splits? Why?). Explain why your chosen method is appropriate or use more than one method as appropriate. Argue why your cross validation method is a realistic mirroring of how an algorithm would be used in practice. Use the method to split your data that you argue for.

The method we use are using for dividing our data into training and testing segements is the Stratified 10-fold cross validation.

Kfold validation ensures all of the data will be used as apart of validation at some point, while shuffle splits would choose the data randomly each time, meaning that not all the data might be sampled and some of it may be sampled multiple times. With our dataset being fairly balanced and large (but not overly large), we don't believe our dataset presents a challenge which appeals to one over the other, but we think Kfold still has it's advantages. Shuffle splits are best for cutting down on validation time. With extremely large data sets, testing across every single piece of data becomes more and more expensive. While it has some of the same benefits as Kfold validation in how it chooses data from across the dataset, which would give a peak into any issues if one of the iterations were to come back performing poorly, it doesn't have all the benefits that a comprehensive look across all the data would give us. As our data is not so exhorbitantly large that using Kfolds would be prohibitively expensive, the extra benefit we get from testing across the entire dataset, seeing how well the data works for the model, is worth the slightly larger cost to run.

After doing some research, it appears that the curve evaluation is for binary classification tasks. We searched through both the scikit learn documentation and several articles for how to calculate these metric, and none of them seemed to make sense for a multilayer perceptron.

So, we decided to use the same metrics we compared the Deep learning models on to compare the Multi-Layer Perceptron model. We got the Precision, Recall, and F1-Score of each class from the Multi-Layer Perceptron and averaged them out to get the average of each. We also calculated the average scores from our first model, which appears to be the best of the deep learning models. Interestingly, the Deep Learning model appears to be better at Recall, while the Multi Layer Perceptron is better at Precision. As we outlined earlier that Precision is the more important metric, although there is a larger difference in the recall, we believe this is not as important as the slight improvement in Precision gained by the Multi Layer Perceptron.
