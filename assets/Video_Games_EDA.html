Business Understanding¶

Its Purpose: The purpose of the “Video Game Sales” dataset is to store data regarding video games (name, platform, year, genre, publisher) and how well they sold around the world (North America, Europe, Japan, “Other,” and added all together for a total Global sales).

Collection Methods: The dataset (from Kaggle) contains a list of video games with sales greater than 100,000 copies, and it was generated by a scrape of vgchartz.com, which gathers data about these games from the companies and developers producing them. VGChartz sources its data directly from video game companies and their sales in exchange for data on trending sales and other video game metrics.

Why is it interesting to Others and the Prediction Task: Many people like video games. Knowing certain statistics about video game sales may not only be interesting to gamers but also to video game companies. Companies and Indie Developers can use these statistics to predict what types of games are most popular amongst consumers around the world and learn from the highest ranked.

Performance Requirements of a Predictive Model to be Considered Useful: To be considered useful, a predictive model needs to give the correct information based on the input data that we give it. Additionally, if the data that is training the model isn’t consistent and accurate, then the predictions we make will be off, giving us useless information.

Data Types for Each Attribute¶

  • Rank (Ranking of overall sales) - Categorical (Ordered)

  • Name (The games name) - Categorial (Nominal)

  • Platform (Platform of the games release (i.e. PC,PS4, etc.)) - Categorical (Nominal)

  • Year (Year of the game's release) - Categorical (Ordered)

  • Genre (Genre of the game) - Categorical (Nominal)

  • Publisher (Publisher of the game) - Categorical (Nominal)

  • NA_Sales (Sales in North America (in millions)) - Quantitative (discrete)

  • EU_Sales (Sales in Europe (in millions)) - Quantitative (discrete)

  • JP_Sales (Sales in Japan (in millions)) - Quantitative (discrete)

  • Other_Sales (ales in the rest of the world (in millions)) - Quantitative (discrete)

  • Global_Sales (Total worldwide sales) - Quantitative (discrete)

Discussing Relevant Attributes¶

Every attribute listed can be useful for a variety of analyses, aside from perhaps Rank, since we could list the games by global sales and have the same result as listing by rank, with more specific data.

NA_Sales, EU_Sales, JP_Sales, and Other_Sales can be relevant if we wish to break down the larger Global_Sales into different parts, such as if we wish to analyze how a game made in Japan sold more or less copies to Japanese customers compared to those in the United States.

Platform, Year, Genre, and Publisher all relatively serve the same purpose when it comes to how we can use them, and that is to analyze the differences in sales for each category. For example, was the average global sales by Nintendo higher than EA? How were sales in the United States affected by events such as the 2008 Stock Market Crash?

Lastly, the Name attribute would be helpful in either identifying the games or if we wished to see the sales associated with a series, such as games with “Mario” or “Kirby” in the title.

Assessing data quality¶

The data has consistent quantitative data and no qualitative data, making it an ideal dataset to do analysis on, yet there are definitely a couple of issues with data quality in both the years and publisher categories.

There are a number of games with N/A as the producer or year with 10 or so games having an N/A as both their year and producer. This is obviously not ideal and is probably due to a lack of contact with certain retailers that haven’t agreed to explicit deals with them, or a problem importing certain data that has not been properly cleansed. While fairly labor intensive, the best way to get this data, without a doubt, would be to manually search each game to figure out the producer and year of the game. Some of the data is even within the name of the game, such as FIFA 2004 having no year data, which speeds up the process and most of the years/producers are easily accessible online. A final justification for this method is that there isn’t too much N/A data, with only about 1.85% of data or 308 cases with each case warranting a quick search at most to fix.

A minor issue we noted as well was that there were a few formatting errors with some producers in their respective strings, but they are still uniquely identifiable making it a quality-of-life change rather than a data-affecting change.

While analyzing the data, we have also discovered a piece of data that was incorrectly inputted into the dataframe, but that will be shown and discussed in the Data Visualization when we look into release years.

Global Sales by Itself¶

We can also visualize just the global sales by itself with a histogram and box (and whisker) plot. Unfortunately, the vast majority of the data is very close together and the outliers spread very far out. This means that if we were to graph a histogram or boxplot including these outliers, we wouldn't be able to see most of the distribution. One way of taking care of these outliers is to only work with the data that is within three standard deviations of the mean.

The outliers displayed in the boxplot without the original data frame's outliers are simply the new data frame's outliers, with a new mean and standard deviation relative to the filtered data. Below that boxplot is what the boxplot would look like if we kept the original outliers. As we can see, it's very difficult to read, and it would have been the same for the histogram if we left the outliers.

Overall, without the original dataset's outliers, the distribution is right-skewed with a center of around 0.15 million sales. The range goes from 0.1 million (since this dataset only has games with over 100,000 sales) to around 1 million, with the outlier range going from around 1 million to around 5.2 million. The main thing we can infer from this is that the vast majority of games will sell less than one million copies globally.

# Filtering the data to not include outliers, as that just leads to only being able to see ~one bar

There's also the fact that arcade gaming is more popular in Japan compared to the U.S., which may result in fewer games being purchased. On the other hand, it's interesting to see how little EA is selling in Japan. After organizing the dataset and solely looking at EA games, many titles have 0 recorded Japan sales, with many more in the hundreths of a million. Much like before in question 1, we took the average sales of sports and FIFA games per year, as then the number of games published will not interfere with how popular they are. A high number of games in one year where people are less interested might give a higher total global sales than one game in another year where interest is very high.

Sports Games: As we can see in the graph “Avg Sales of Sports Games Globally,” the second most popular year for sports games was 1984—when the Summer Olympics was hosted in Los Angeles, California—which had an average of roughly 3.1 million games sold. The most popular year was the one right before this, with roughly 3.25 million. Right after this, though, the year before the next Olympics experienced a sharp decline in popularity compared to the years surrounding it, with around 1 million in 1987 and around 1.75 million in 1988. (The extremely high numbers for those 1983–4 are due to the only sports games being very popular NES releases, and there weren’t any other games to lower the average.) After this, four Olympics had better sales than the year before it, with only two having less in 2008 (The U.S. stock market crashed that September, so that might have been a reason for fewer sales) and 2016. This implies that overall, years that host the Olympics increase the public’s interest in sports, resulting in a rise in sports games, even if slight.

FIFA Games: Something important to note about FIFA games and their releases, they are released the year before the dates of their games, like FIFA 2016 would come out in 2015. Additionally, FIFA 2000 was also released in 1998 for some reason, yet the amount of sales within that period is still significant as FIFA 2000’s European sales are what raises the graph for the year.

We initially believed that the sales of FIFA games in particular would increase during the World Cup, when the people’s game truly shines and the passion of fans can be fully enjoyed on its virtual counterpart. This might especially be the case for Americans who follow less closely, yet still get the urge to have interest following an especially exciting tournament. This was not the case, as there seemingly is no correlation between World Cup years and not, a quite surprising metric. There is a special mode in FIFA about the World Cup, so an increase in sales makes additional sense, yet to no help in trending sales. The real standout year, in general, was FIFA 2016 (2015), yet there was no World Cup that year, and the impact on the Euros and Copa America (two tournaments in respective continents) wouldn’t have such an impact.

Something interesting to note is the disparity between European purchases and American purchases throughout. In the early times of FIFA’s release, with the major exception of FIFA 2000 in 1998, America outsold Europe despite a lack of interest in the sport in comparison. This was most likely due to the higher access to consoles and capable hardware to run on computers. Yet, past 2002, Europe continuously outsole the US by considerable margins, with the major trend of differences starting in 2010. With the increasing globality of gaming in general, FIFA was one of the first games picked up in European countries whilst the US didn’t have much interest besides dedicated fans of the game.

There is a lot to be determined from this simple display of data, with the ability to create a video game timeline that correlates with the technological and economic advances possible. The reason averages per year for the top 100 games each year were picked as the data to represent this timeline is because having sums of all of the games each year and averaging that would put the data more slanted against more recent games where games are generally more accessible to make. Global sales for each year in general would not work for the same reason, as with more games there is likely to be more sales overall and we want to measure more of the general population's trends on this topic rather than the more indie side.

The early 1980s were a time when household computers were finally being established and the video game industry was but a child. Around this time, the technology industry in the US was competing with Russia to be the first on the moon which led to many other advancements in adjacent fields, like video games. This growth was followed by the console crash of 1983 where terrible games were released quickly in what seemed to be a profitable market for the Atari. This is directly correlated to the economic recession in 1983, where people were so focused on making money that producers were rushed and people generally had less money to spend. In late 1985, the NES was released which had some all-time classic games, leading to a reconnaissance in 1986. The industry generally fluctuated as new consoles were released until a somewhat steady playing field of competition between SONY, Xbox, and Nintendo consoles. The next couple of years were less eventful than the 80s for the gaming industry and overall, represented by fairly steady sales with a slight drop off between 1997 - 1998 being due to transitions between consoles. Post 2000 becomes more eventful with 9/11 coming fairly early to throw a wrench into global politics. The economic impacts of this awful event led to a fear of buying from foreign producers, which were the primary game producers via Nintendo and Sony, leading to a significant fall in sales in 2002. That was abruptly shifted by the release of many cult classics in 2003, like Halo 2 and Call of Duty (a great release time following the political tension) which shifted views towards the escapism of video games. The economic recession of 2008 can also be seen quite visibly in the chart, with 2007 being a standout year for gaming following the release of the global phenomenon that is the Wii in late 2006. After this massive wave of purchases and less purchasing power in total, sales plummeted as people were unable to spend on luxuries.

With the release of consoles, the influx of purchases mostly came in the year following their release and with increased certainty when other consoles were released in competition with each other. For instance, if we look at the stand-out year, 2007, the Xbox 360, PlayStation 2, and Wii were all released in a short timeframe. The sales for games were expected to skyrocket in general as everyone was getting the big games for their console's respective release. This isn't a one-time relation either, with the spike in sales in 2014 being indicative of the release of both the Xbox One and PS4 with an influx of gamers getting one of the two.
